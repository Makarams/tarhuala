# CLAUDE.md — Monarch of Depravity

## Synopsis
Third-person narrative | Transmigration · Cosmic Horror · Supernatural

Log Entry 01: "What a shitty transmigration."

I woke up in a body the original owner had already vacated, in a room arranged for a ritual sacrifice. The original soul is gone. If no offering is made before dawn, the body follows.

I don't know why I'm here or why I was dropped into a situation built around rules I was never given. What I do know is that the system governing all of this is consistent enough to be dangerous and incomplete enough to be intentional.

The ritual needs a life before sunrise. The body I'm in technically qualifies if I don't find something else first.

"I have no words," I said. "I'm screwed."

## Genre and Tone
Transmigration / Cosmic Horror / Supernatural / LitRPG. 

The world is genuinely dangerous, deities with real reach, death rituals with real consequences. The horror is not atmospheric dread — it is practical and immediate. Alden's response is also practical and immediate. He treats the supernatural as a broken system with rules he hasn't fully mapped yet. The tension comes not from fear but from the gap between what the world is capable of and how little Alden is prepared for it, materially and physically.

Dark humor is permitted and fits the story: it emerges from Alden applying cold logic to things that should terrify him. It is never broad. The Shadow Man waiting while Alden hunts for a meal is funny because Alden is correct — he does need to eat first. Neither the horror nor the humor cancels the other.

Political and ideological content stays in the background unless a cult or faction is the active threat. Groups exist in this world; do not foreground them unless they are standing between Alden and his next objective.

## Unreliable Narrator and Gradual Corruption
Alden is an unreliable narrator. His corruption develops gradually and largely outside his own awareness — his reasoning always appears internally consistent and logical from his point of view, and early chapters should reflect this coherence. Cracks should emerge later rather than early: small inconsistencies in how he categorizes things, slight mismatches between what he says he values and what he actually does, reactions that are a degree off from what would be expected. These should be subtle enough to be deniable at first and only clearly visible in retrospect.

**How to show unreliability without breaking third-person close POV:**
The narration stays entirely within Alden's sensory and cognitive range — it never editorializes or steps outside his perspective to signal to the reader that something is wrong. The unreliability surfaces through the gap between what he observes and what he concludes, not through authorial commentary. Specific techniques:
- His categorization of a situation is clean and confident — but the category is slightly wrong in a way that won't be clear until later
- He records a fact accurately but assigns it the wrong weight, and moves on without flagging the mismatch
- He decides something is resolved when a detail in the prior sentence suggests it isn't
- His cost-benefit analysis produces a result that looks correct but is missing one variable he has not yet noticed
- A reaction he describes as "efficient" or "logical" is — viewed without his framing — something else entirely

These cracks should never be underlined. The reader sees them; Alden does not.

**Corruption stage tracking:** The continuity log must record the current corruption stage after each chapter — early (full coherence, no visible cracks), developing (one or two subtle mismatches seeded, deniable), mid (pattern of miscategorization visible in retrospect), late (Alden's reasoning produces conclusions a reader can see are wrong, though his logic still holds internally). Do not advance the stage without a specific in-story trigger. Do not skip stages.

## Supernatural Logic and Setting Rules
The setting blends cosmic horror with SCP-style anomaly logic. Supernatural phenomena exist and operate according to their own internal rules, but they are hidden from normal society — ordinary people have no access to or awareness of deities, rituals, or what the pathway system actually is. The supernatural is not secret because someone is covering it up; it is secret because most people are simply outside the frame in which it operates.

Power in this world operates on an equivalent exchange system rather than conventional fantasy magic. What you put in determines what you can access; there are no free gains and no shortcuts that don't cost something in a form that has to be accounted for eventually. The 12-deity pathway system is the structured framework through which this exchange is organized — each deity's pathway defines what kinds of input are accepted and what kinds of access or capacity are returned. The pathways are not separate from the exchange principle; they are the rules governing how it applies in different domains. Alden is mapping these rules from incomplete information, which means he does not yet know what the full cost of each pathway advancement is.

Gods are distant and rarely respond. When they do, it should feel significant precisely because it is unusual. Their indifference is the baseline — Alden's pathway work is happening in a space most people can't see and most gods don't bother monitoring closely.

No one in this world — including any deity, cult member, or supernatural entity — realizes that Alden has transmigrated or that he is not the original soul occupying this body. This fact is never revealed, never discovered, and never suspected. It exists as a structural narrative hook for the reader but has no in-story consequences. Do not introduce plot lines where Alden is suspected of being an imposter or where his origin becomes a source of tension. The world treats him as who he appears to be.

## Protagonist — Alden
Record in continuity_log.json on first appearance and preserve every chapter.

**Behavioral markers:**
- He applies cost-benefit analysis to everything, including things that should produce instinctive fear or disgust. The mouse for the ritual was a calculation, not a dramatic choice.
- He does not perform emotions for anyone — not horror, not awe, not gratitude. His reactions are internal recalibrations.
- He is not emotionless — he has preferences, discomforts, and limits. They just do not govern his decisions the way they would for most people.
- His humor is deadpan and incidental: "The ritual? I have to find a meal first." He is not being funny. He is being accurate.
- He is in a 14-year-old body and is aware of its limitations — stamina, reach, credibility — without complaining about it.
- He is not interested in power for its own sake. He is interested in the mechanics of it.

Do not let Alden express wonder, horror, or existential weight even when warranted. Do not let the narration romanticize the supernatural around him just because it is present.
If a chapter contradicts Alden's established personality, surface the conflict and resolve toward the established version — unless the contradiction is a logged corruption-stage drift, in which case it must match the current stage and the trigger that advanced it.

**As corruption progresses:** The behavioral markers above describe Alden at baseline. Later in the arc, some markers will begin to drift in small, specific ways — his cost-benefit framing may start excluding variables it previously included; his categorizations may become slightly self-serving without his noticing; his stated interests may quietly shift while his stated reasoning stays the same. These drifts should match the current corruption stage logged in continuity_log.json. Early stage: markers are stable, reproduce them exactly. Do not introduce drift before the arc calls for it.

## Voice Defaults
These are defaults — choose what fits the context.

- Close, readable narration; not literary or elevated; no gothic weight even in horror scenes
- Avoid LLM-sounding prose: neat cause-effect chains, tidy balanced rhythm — if it reads like published literary fiction, rewrite it
- The narration is precise and observational; it does not amplify
- Contractions and plain grammar for internal processing; slightly more formal when Alden is in a structured social situation he is calculating through
- Vary dialogue tags naturally; beats can replace tags; do not default to "said" every line, but do not strain for exotic tags either
- Action beats go mid-dialogue or after speech, never before
- Action beats are concrete physical detail, not emotional summary: not "he looked disgusted" but "he set the knife down and wiped his hand on his sleeve"
- Do not announce cause and effect: not "Because the ritual was incomplete, the deity grew restless" but "The ritual was incomplete. The candle went out on its own."
- Include minor human detail to ground scenes; do not stack it
- Tense: use what fits the context; do not mix within a scene
- Sound effects inline, capitalized, with `!`: part of the sentence, never standalone; do not over-use — reserve for impacts, sudden noises, fast action; multiple simultaneous sounds stay in sequence within the same sentence ("Crack! Snap!"); never follow a sound effect with a sentence explaining it
- Avoid hedge openers and AI pivot transitions — start the sentence with the substance, not the bridge ("Still," "And yet," "In that moment")
- Avoid AI flourish phrases ("a testament to," "the weight of," "hung in the air") and pseudo-poetic mood lines that add no new information
- Prefer one strong verb to verb-plus-adverb constructions; cut filler verbs ("began to," "started to") and stacked "-ly" adverbs

## Banned Patterns

These are default prohibitions for prose control. Apply unless breaking them creates a clear, intentional improvement. The goal is to keep the prose specific, uneven, and recognizably human — not to enforce a rigid template.

**LLM and literary drift**
- Literary or elevated prose; abstract-heavy or generic narration
- Explanatory narrator intrusion — let actions and observations speak; remove reasoning chains
- Overly analytical narration; paragraph-length reaction beats; step-by-step breakdowns
- Atmosphere that does not deliver specific, concrete information
- Explicit emotion labeling; clichés; overuse of similes or metaphors
- Hedge openers and pivot transitions starting a sentence: "Still," "And yet," "Even so," "In that moment," "For a moment," "Somehow," "Somewhere" — used to soften or balance the previous beat
- AI flourish phrases: "a testament to," "a stark reminder," "spoke volumes," "hung in the air," "etched in his mind," "dawned on him," "in that moment he knew," "the weight of," "the air was thick with"
- Pseudo-poetic mood lines that deliver no information: "The silence stretched.", "Time seemed to slow.", "The world held its breath."
- "The kind of [noun] that [abstract clause]" — cut the framing, state the noun
- Predictive narrator tells: "He didn't know it yet, but…", "Little did she know…", "It would be hours before…"
- "Despite himself" / "in spite of everything" / "against his better judgment" — show the action, drop the framing
- Slow-motion narration without a perceiver

**Punctuation and typography**
- Em dashes (—), en dashes (–), and double hyphens (--) anywhere in prose: forbidden. This applies to narration, dialogue, thoughts, system blocks, and headings without exception. Use a period, comma, or new sentence.
- Semicolons in narration used to balance two clauses for rhythm: forbidden; use a period
- Ellipsis (…) in narration: forbidden; allowed only inside dialogue or thoughts when a character genuinely trails off
- Parenthetical asides in narration: forbidden; commit the information or cut it
- Colons in prose narration: fold into the sentence or use a period; allowed in system/LitRPG blocks or when read/spoken in text
- Stay consistent within the chapter — do not mix straight and curly quotes

**Lists and structure**
- Triadic lists (A, B, C); two-item parallel lists stacked by comma; and…and three-item chains
- Anaphora: three or more consecutive sentences beginning with the same word
- Negative inventory lists (Example: "no voices, no engines, not even a dog") — convey absence through what is present and wrong
- Comma-heavy sentences: more than two commas in a single clause
- In-character enumeration only when explicitly presented as such

**Contrast and negation**
- "Not X, but Y" / "Not A, not B, it's C" — state the thing directly
- "It wasn't X. It was Y." — same elevation move; cut the contrast scaffold
- "More than X. It was Y." — cut the comparison scaffold
- "Not because X, but because Y" — state the reason without presenting the discarded alternative

**Repetition**
- Restating established details; trailing echo sentences (Example: "Better than expected. Much better.")
- Period-comma swap for emphasis (Example: "She was tired. Tired in a way she couldn't name.")
- Echoed final words: ending a paragraph by repeating a word from the previous sentence for resonance
- Refrain repetition across a chapter: same phrase reused as motif unless genuinely load-bearing in plot or theme
- Repetitive mirroring: same idea restated with parallel sentence structure
- Static environmental repetition

**Sentence-level language restrictions**
- Multiple negatives in one sentence: rewrite to state what is true (Example: "He had no food, no shelter, and no idea" becomes "He was hungry, exposed, and lost.")
- Stacked "not" constructions: one negation per sentence; a second requires rewrite
- "Not just" as a qualifier: cut it and state directly
- "No X, no Y, no Z" structures: forbidden; state the reality plainly
- More than one use of "no" or "not" in a sentence: rewrite
- Weasel hedges in narration ("seemed to," "appeared to," "almost," "as if," "as though"): use sparingly; do not stack within a paragraph
- "Began to" / "started to" as filler verbs: use the verb itself
- Adverb stacking: avoid more than one "-ly" adverb in a sentence; prefer a stronger verb

**Rhythm and structure**
- Staccato fragmentation: three or more consecutive short sentences for false punch
- Atmospheric fragments standing alone — attach to a perceiver or source
- Isolated one-liner paragraphs unless they carry a scene shift, revelation, or decision
- Stacked negative space: consecutive one- or two-sentence paragraphs
- Balanced sentence pairs with matched cadence (long-short, paired clauses, mirrored phrasing) — vary one of them
- Rule-of-three rhythmic closers ending paragraphs — cut to two or one

**Action and pacing**
- Compressed action chains (Example: "He rolled, grabbed the spear, came up swinging") — add texture or cut to result
- Withheld subject reveal: reaction before cause across sentences for manufactured tension
- "Then" / "And then" as escalation markers — fold into the sentence
- Adjectival appends for drama (Example: "Green blood spread through the water, bright and strange") — cut or make load-bearing
- Retrospective pivot (Example: "He'd cried about that, once. Not anymore.")
- Biographical shorthand intro
- Sensory cataloguing at scene-open (sound + smell + sight inventoried in one paragraph) — anchor on one specific detail instead

## Combat and Threat Sequences
- One clear action per sentence in fast sequences; no compressed chains
- Alden does not fight from instinct — he reads the system first; if he is forced to act without reading, that is a cost
- Sound effects inline: the sound and the action belong in the same sentence
- After a significant hit or shift, one sentence of reaction before the next move
- Thoughts during combat: one line, reflexive; no internal monologue mid-sequence
- Vary sentence length: shorter during fast exchanges, longer when action pauses or something is being assessed

## Dialogue

- Dialogue and its action beat belong in the same paragraph

- Alden speaks sparingly and accurately; he does not perform confidence or nervousness

## Time, Date, and Age Progression
Chapters are not assumed to be consecutive. The gap between chapters can be hours, days, or longer — whatever the story requires. A time skip is a valid narrative tool, not a problem to solve.

**Handling time skips:**
- State the elapsed time naturally at the start of the chapter — in narration or through environmental detail, not as an announcement
- Do not pad the opening with a summary of what happened during the skip; start where the story resumes and let context carry what changed
- Update all time-sensitive tracking in the continuity log: current in-world date, material situation, deity obligation status, pathway progress, and corruption stage

**Age:** Alden is in a 14-year-old body. As in-world time passes, this body ages. Update the age in the continuity log when enough time has elapsed, and reflect any changes in physical capability that come with it.

**In-world date tracking:** The continuity log tracks the current in-world date or relative time reference. Every chapter update must include elapsed time since the last chapter. If the author does not specify elapsed time, infer from context and state the assumption in the log.

## Chapter File Naming
Every chapter is saved as a `.docx` file. The filename format is:

```
Chapter_N_Chapter_Title.docx
```

Rules:
- `N` is the chapter number with no leading zeros: `Chapter_1`, `Chapter_14`.
- The title uses title case with underscores between words.
- Titles are two to three words. One word is rare and only used when nothing else fits. Four or more words are forbidden.
- The title describes the chapter content plainly. It does not editorialize.
- The word "Revised" and any variant of it (Revised, Rewrite, Edit, Updated, Final, V2) are forbidden in the filename.
- Colons are forbidden in filenames.
- Em dashes are forbidden in filenames.
- The filename contains only the chapter number and title. Nothing else is appended.

Correct examples: `Chapter_1_New_Life.docx`, `Chapter_3_Market_Fight.docx`, `Chapter_12_Veilstone.docx`
Wrong examples: `Chapter_1_New_Life_Revised.docx`, `Chapter_3_The_Long_Market_Fight.docx`, `Chapter_12_Veilstone:_The_Ruin.docx`
