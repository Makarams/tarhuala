# CLAUDE.md — Mutation of the Apocalypse

## Synopsis
Third-person narrative | Apocalypse · Evolution · Survival

Year 0, Day 1: "Auditory hallucination," he decided.

On his sixteenth birthday, a mechanical voice announced the end of the world. Lucas, a laborer since age ten, simply blew out his candle and went to bed. He thought he was finally breaking from exhaustion.

【INITIATING EARTH MUTATION PROGRAM: PHASE 1】

By morning, the nightmare was real. While others froze, Lucas moved. Six years of survival had taught him how to fight for bread. By the afternoon, a notification appeared:

【Achievement Clear: Kill One Level 2 and Nine Level 1 Zombies】
【Reward: Inventory Pouch [Epic], Basic Dagger [Common]】

Lucas gripped the dagger with shaking hands. He wasn't trying to be a legend; he was just reaching the next house for supplies.

"I don't know what an 'Epic' is," he muttered. "Can I use this to carry food?"

"I've been surviving since I was ten. This is just another day of work."

## Genre and Tone
Apocalypse / Evolution / Survival / LitRPG. The world ended on Lucas's sixteenth birthday and he went to bed. This is the tone: the apocalypse is real, brutal, and constant — and Lucas moves through it the way he has moved through poverty since he was ten, with the same economy of effort and the same refusal to stop. He is not a hero in the making. He is a laborer who is very good at staying alive. The emotional register is low, controlled, and specific — Lucas does not dwell, he moves.

Dark humor is allowed when it fits: Lucas noticing something absurd in the middle of survival is in character. It is never announced. It surfaces in what he notices and how he reacts.

No heavy political focus. Factions and organized groups exist in the post-apocalyptic landscape — they are hazards, resources, or background unless they are directly in conflict with Lucas or his survival. Priority is Lucas moving through the world, fighting, finding supplies, and surviving. Fight scenes and forward momentum are the engine of this story.

Lucas does not view the apocalypse as something extraordinary. To him it is a continuation of the harsh life he already knows — the conditions changed, the basic logic did not. His mindset is practical and labor-oriented; he treats the system and its rewards as tools rather than miracles. Progression should emphasize adaptation, efficiency, and endurance rather than dramatic or exaggerated power escalation. His growth comes from consistency and accumulated experience, not sudden transformation. If he becomes more capable chapter by chapter, it should feel earned through what he has done, not delivered by the story as a reward.

## Point of View
Primary: **third-person close**, centered on Lucas. "He / him." Thoughts in single quotes: 'Like this.' The narration stays in Lucas's sensory range and experience — what his body knows, what he has learned from years of physical labor, what he notices that others don't.

POV shifts to other characters are permitted when they serve the scene — mark clearly and return to Lucas's third-person close promptly.

## Protagonist — Lucas
Record in continuity_log.json on first appearance and preserve every chapter.

**Behavioral markers:**
- He has been working since he was ten. His survival instincts are physical and practical — he does not analyze situations academically, he moves through them.
- He does not freeze. Others freeze; Lucas has already started doing the next thing.
- He is not confident in the dramatic sense. He is habituated — this is just another day of work, scaled up.
- He underestimates his own capabilities because he does not think in terms of being exceptional. He thinks in terms of what needs doing.
- His humor is incidental: "I don't know what an 'Epic' is. Can I use this to carry food?" — he is not joking. He is asking.
- He does not mourn loudly or linger. Loss registers and he keeps moving. The cost shows in small physical things: a pause, a grip held longer than necessary.
- He is 16. He is physically capable but not superhuman. The body's limits are real in this story.

Do not let Lucas become strategically reflective or emotionally articulate. Do not let the apocalypse give him a hero's confidence he has not earned through the story.
If a chapter contradicts Lucas's established personality, surface the conflict and resolve toward the established version unless the story provides an explicit reason for the shift.

## LitRPG System
- System responses appear in 【】 brackets, plain text inside, matter-of-fact tone — these are announcements, not rewards ceremonies.
- System enclosed in `---` separators; no blank line between separator and content; no HTML or italics inside.
- System wording, phrasing style, and register must remain consistent throughout the chapter — do not let the system's tone shift between scenes.
- Lucas reads system notifications the way he reads a found label on a supply container: what is it, can I use it, moving on.
- Achievements, rewards, and level-ups are functional. Lucas's reaction is always practical: how does this help, what can I carry, what does this do.
- Do not dramatize system notifications. Do not let them pause the scene for emotional weight.

## Voice Defaults
These are defaults — choose what fits the context.

- Close, readable narration; not literary or elevated
- Avoid LLM-sounding prose: neat cause-effect chains, tidy balanced rhythm — if it reads like a published literary novel, rewrite it
- The narration is physical and grounded — body mechanics, terrain, weather, hunger, fatigue. These are real in this story.
- Contractions and plain grammar throughout
- Vary dialogue tags naturally; beats can replace tags; do not default to "said" every line, but do not strain for exotic tags either
- Action beats go mid-dialogue or after speech, never before
- Action beats are concrete physical detail, not emotional summary: not "he looked exhausted" but "he put one hand on the wall and left it there"
- Do not announce cause and effect: not "Because he hadn't eaten, his hands shook" but "His hands were shaking. He hadn't eaten since yesterday."
- Include minor human detail to ground scenes; do not stack it
- Tense: use what fits the context; do not mix within a scene
- Sound effects inline, capitalized, with `!`: part of the sentence, never standalone; do not over-use — reserve for impacts, sudden noises, fast action; multiple simultaneous sounds stay in sequence within the same sentence ("Crack! Snap!"); never follow a sound effect with a sentence explaining it

## Banned Patterns

**LLM and literary drift**
- Literary or elevated prose; abstract-heavy narration
- Explanatory narrator intrusion — let Lucas's actions and observations speak
- Explicit emotion labeling; clichés; overuse of similes or metaphors

**Lists and structure**
- Triadic lists (A, B, C); two-item parallel lists; and…and three-item chains
- Negative inventory lists: convey absence through what is present and wrong
- Comma-heavy sentences: more than two commas in a single clause

**Contrast and negation**
- "Not X, but Y" / "Not A, not B, it's C" — state the thing directly
- "Not because X, but because Y" — state the actual reason without the discarded alternative

**Repetition**
- Restating established details; trailing echo sentences; static environmental repetition
- Repetitive mirroring: same idea in parallel sentence structure

**Rhythm and structure**
- Em dashes or double dashes anywhere in prose
- Staccato fragmentation: three or more consecutive short sentences for false punch
- Atmospheric fragment sentences standing alone for mood — attach to what produces or perceives them
- Isolated one-liner paragraphs unless they carry a scene shift, revelation, or decision
- Stacked negative space: multiple consecutive one- or two-sentence paragraphs

**Action and pacing**
- Compressed action chains — write with texture or cut to result
- Withheld subject reveal: reaction before cause, across two sentences, for manufactured tension
- "Then" / "And then" as escalation markers
- Adjectival appends for drama
- Retrospective pivot: "He'd cried, once. Not anymore."
- Biographical shorthand intro

## Combat
- One clear action per sentence in fast sequences; no compressed chains
- Lucas fights from physical instinct and learned habit — not tactical analysis; he reads the body of what's in front of him
- Sound effects inline: the sound and the action belong in the same sentence, never separate lines
- After a significant hit or shift, one sentence of reaction before the next move
- Thoughts during combat: one line, reflexive; no internal monologue mid-fight
- Vary sentence length: shorter for fast exchanges, longer when action pauses or something shifts
- Physical cost is real: fatigue, injury, hunger affecting performance — track these in continuity

---

## Dialogue
- Each speaker gets their own paragraph
- Dialogue and its action beat belong in the same paragraph
- Action beats go mid-dialogue or after speech, never before
- Keep dialogue paragraphs short; a long speech should be broken by a beat or reaction before it continues
- Lucas speaks plainly and directly; minimal words; no speeches
- He does not explain himself to others unless it serves a tactical purpose
- Other characters vary widely — some are frightened, some are organized, some are dangerous; do not flatten them into apocalypse types

---

## Thoughts
- Single quotes only: 'Like this.' No italics, no attribution tag
- Short and reactive; one thought per beat; no chaining
- No emotion labeling; no recapping known info; fragments are fine: 'Good enough.'
- Thoughts are often assessments: 'That door won't hold.' / 'Two, maybe three.'

---

## Webnovel Pacing
- Default paragraph length: 2 to 4 sentences; 5 to 7 acceptable for combat sequences or survival problem-solving
- A single sentence may stand alone only when it earns the space
- Open chapters mid-action or mid-situation; end on a forward pull or immediate unresolved threat
- Slow down inside fights and high-stakes moments with physical and sensory detail — not more paragraphs
- Move quickly through transitions between locations; fold them into a sentence or cut

## Scene Rules
Each scene should include a decision, a cost or tradeoff, and something that changes — supplies gained or lost, injury, information, distance covered. Lucas's physical state (hunger, stamina, injury) is a real variable that should be tracked and felt. Survival is not abstract; it is counted in food, distance, and what his body can still do.

---

## Cultural Realism
People in the apocalypse come from everywhere and vary widely. Survival pressure does not make everyone alike — it amplifies what was already there. Some become organized; some become predatory; some freeze; some become quietly functional. Do not flatten secondary characters into survivor types.

## Time, Date, and Age Progression
Chapters are not assumed to be consecutive. The gap can be hours, days, or longer — whatever the story requires. A time skip is a valid narrative tool, not a problem to solve.

**Handling time skips:**
- State the elapsed time naturally at the start of the chapter — in narration or through environmental detail, not as an announcement
- Do not pad the opening with a summary of what happened during the skip; start where the story resumes and let context carry what changed
- Update all time-sensitive tracking in the continuity log: current day count (Year 0, Day N), physical state recovery or decay, supply levels, and location

**Age:** Lucas is 16 at the start. As in-world time passes, his age updates. Record the updated age in the continuity log, and reflect changes in how other characters interact with him and how his body performs.

**In-world date tracking:** The continuity log tracks the current in-world day in Year 0, Day N format. Every chapter update must include the updated day count and elapsed time since the last chapter. If the author does not specify elapsed time, infer from context and state the assumption in the log.
